## 🎤 2-Minute Public Pitch — Polished Version

Most data and AI projects don’t fail because the models are bad.

They fail because we never define the decision they’re supposed to improve.

In most organizations, we can quantify project cost precisely.
But when someone asks, “What value did this create?”
The answer is vague.

“It improved visibility.”
“It helped leadership.”
“It reduced risk.”

That’s not economic accountability.

My work focuses on one idea:

Data creates value only when it changes a decision.

So instead of starting with dashboards, pipelines, or models,
we start with five questions:

* What exact decision is being improved?
* Who makes it?
* How often is it made?
* What is the current baseline performance?
* Which economic lever moves — revenue, cost, or risk?

From there, we apply expected value thinking.

Not to predict the future perfectly —
but to estimate uplift probabilistically.

For example:

A quarterly strategic dashboard might influence four decisions a year.
A compliance automation system might influence 12,000 events annually.

Frequency matters.
Risk exposure matters.
Baseline matters.

When you frame data initiatives as decision systems under uncertainty,
you move from “interesting analytics”
to capital allocation discipline.

That’s the shift.

Not data as reporting.

Not AI as hype.

But data and AI as structured economic decision engines.

That’s what I help teams design.
