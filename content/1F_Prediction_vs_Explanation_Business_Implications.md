From first principles, a prediction answers the question: *What is likely to happen?* An explanation answers: *Why does it happen?* These are related but fundamentally different objectives. Prediction is about estimating future states with acceptable accuracy. Explanation is about identifying mechanisms that connect cause and effect.

A system can predict without understanding. Weather models forecast rain based on patterns in atmospheric data, even if every micro-level interaction is not fully explained. Similarly, a machine learning model can predict customer churn with high accuracy by detecting patterns in behavior. But knowing that a customer is likely to leave is different from knowing why they are leaving.

This distinction matters economically. If a bank’s model predicts higher default probability for a segment, the institution can price loans accordingly. That is a predictive use. However, if leaders want to reduce defaults, they need explanation. Are customers defaulting due to income shocks, poor underwriting, or product misalignment? Without understanding the mechanism, interventions may be ineffective or misdirected.

Prediction optimizes short-term decision efficiency. Explanation improves strategic control. A retailer might predict declining sales next quarter using historical data. That helps with inventory planning. But explaining the decline — perhaps due to shifting customer preferences or competitive entry — enables product redesign or repositioning. Explanation supports scenario planning because it clarifies which variables, if changed, would alter outcomes.

Machine learning models often prioritize predictive performance over interpretability. Complex models can capture subtle nonlinear relationships but may obscure causal pathways. Integrating explanation — through interpretable models, feature analysis, experiments, or domain reasoning — reduces overreliance on black-box outputs. It allows leaders to assess whether predictions will hold under changing conditions.

The business risk of relying only on prediction is fragility. If underlying mechanisms shift, predictive accuracy may degrade quickly. When explanation is incorporated, organizations better understand structural drivers and can adapt proactively.

In practice, strong decision systems use both. Prediction allocates resources efficiently in the present. Explanation informs strategy, risk management, and long-term investment. One answers “What should we expect?” The other answers “What can we change?”

Together, they transform data from a forecasting tool into a lever for durable advantage under uncertainty.
