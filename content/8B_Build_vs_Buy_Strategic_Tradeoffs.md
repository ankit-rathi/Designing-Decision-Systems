## B. Build vs Buy — Strategic Tradeoffs

### Why

Engineering resources are scarce. Time-to-market matters. Control matters.

Every build vs buy decision is a bet on where your competitive edge lives.

### What

* **Build**: custom, differentiated capability
* **Buy**: standardized, commoditized capability

### How

Ask:

1. Is this core to our competitive advantage?
2. Will owning it improve margins or defensibility?
3. Is the vendor solution “good enough”?

Build differentiation.
Buy infrastructure.

Confuse the two → waste capital.

---

From first principles, a firm has limited capital, limited engineering bandwidth, and limited time. Every hour spent building something is an hour not spent building something else. Therefore, build-versus-buy is fundamentally a capital allocation decision under constraint.

The core question is: where does competitive advantage originate? If an activity meaningfully differentiates the firm — improving margins, defensibility, customer experience, or strategic control — owning it may compound value over time. If an activity is standardized and widely available, building it internally consumes resources without creating edge.

Building means creating custom capability tailored to the firm’s strategy. For example, a logistics company whose advantage depends on route optimization may invest in proprietary optimization algorithms. That capability directly affects cost structure and service quality. Owning it strengthens margins and creates defensibility because competitors cannot easily replicate the exact system.

Buying means adopting a vendor solution that is standardized and “good enough.” Payroll systems, cloud hosting, CRM platforms, and basic analytics tooling rarely create differentiation. They are infrastructure. Attempting to build them in-house typically delays execution and increases maintenance burden without strategic upside.

Time-to-market also matters. In fast-moving industries, speed can outweigh customization. If a vendor provides a solution that covers 80–90% of needs immediately, buying may allow the company to focus scarce talent on higher-leverage initiatives. Delayed differentiation is often worse than imperfect infrastructure.

Control and long-term cost must also be considered. Building provides deeper customization and potentially lower marginal cost at scale, but introduces maintenance risk and opportunity cost. Buying reduces operational complexity but may create vendor dependency or pricing power imbalance. The decision hinges on whether ownership meaningfully shifts the firm’s long-term economics.

Three filtering questions clarify the tradeoff. First: Is this capability central to our competitive advantage? Second: Will owning it materially improve margins or defensibility? Third: Is the vendor solution sufficiently robust for our needs?

The principle is simple but often violated. Build what differentiates. Buy what commoditizes. When organizations build infrastructure that does not create edge, they dilute focus and waste capital. When they outsource core differentiation, they surrender advantage.

Strategic clarity in build-versus-buy decisions preserves scarce resources for what truly compounds value.
