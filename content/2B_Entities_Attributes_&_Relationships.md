## **B. Entities, Attributes & Relationships**

**Why:** You can’t analyze what you don’t define. Decisions are made about identifiable objects or processes.

**What:** Entities are **units of analysis**, attributes are **their measurable properties**, and relationships show **how entities interact**.

**How:** Build models linking entities and attributes. For instance, customers (entity) with purchase history (attributes) interacting with products (relationships) reveal actionable patterns.

---

From first principles, analysis requires structure. Reality is continuous and complex, but decision-making demands abstraction. To reason effectively, we must define *what* we are analyzing and *how* its components relate. Without clear definitions, data becomes noise and conclusions become arbitrary.

An **entity** is a distinct unit of analysis — something about which a decision can be made. A customer, product, supplier, loan, or transaction can all be entities. Entities anchor accountability and action. If you cannot point to the object of analysis, you cannot intervene meaningfully.

An **attribute** is a measurable property of an entity. For a customer, attributes may include age, purchase frequency, average order value, or churn probability. Attributes convert qualitative reality into quantifiable signals. They allow comparison, segmentation, and prediction. However, attributes only gain meaning when tied to a defined entity.

**Relationships** describe how entities interact. A customer purchases a product. A supplier delivers to a warehouse. A borrower repays a loan. These interactions form networks that often contain more insight than attributes alone. For example, two customers with identical purchase frequency may differ in product relationships — one buys high-margin items, another low-margin items. The economic implication differs.

Consider a retail system. If you only analyze products as isolated entities with sales volume attributes, you miss cross-product relationships. But if you map customer-product relationships, patterns emerge: customers who buy product A frequently buy product B. This insight enables bundling, recommendation systems, and targeted promotions.

In credit risk, a loan is an entity with attributes such as interest rate, tenure, and borrower income. But relationships — such as shared employer networks or geographic clusters — may reveal correlated risk exposures invisible at the individual level.

Structuring data around entities, attributes, and relationships transforms raw information into analyzable systems. It clarifies what decisions apply to which units, what properties influence outcomes, and how interactions propagate effects.

Poorly defined entities lead to duplicated records and inconsistent metrics. Missing relationships hide systemic risk. Weak attribute design distorts modeling.

Effective decision systems begin with disciplined modeling of entities, their measurable characteristics, and the relationships connecting them. Once structure is explicit, analysis becomes coherent, and interventions become targeted rather than diffuse.
